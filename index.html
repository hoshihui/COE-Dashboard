Problem Statement

Singapore’s COE system is complex, with prices influenced by quota supply, vehicle demand, government policies, and market sentiment. Information is fragmented across multiple sources, making it difficult for buyers, sellers, dealers, and policymakers to understand trends, assess affordability, or anticipate market shifts. Stakeholders face challenges such as identifying the most affordable periods, comparing COE premiums across categories and bidding groups, evaluating price volatility, and understanding car ownership and retention behaviors. The lack of an integrated, data-driven view limits decision-making and increases financial and strategic risks. These dashboards aim to consolidate COE data into actionable insights, allowing stakeholders to track trends, compare options, and make informed decisions efficiently.

Overview

Vehicle demand in Singapore remains strong despite rising COE and a clear shift from fuel-based to electric and hybrid vehicles.

Overview Insights

The line chart shows a general increase in both COE premiums and vehicle registrations, indicating strong and resilient demand for cars in Singapore despite rising ownership costs. The fact that registrations continued to grow alongside higher COE premiums suggests that many consumers were willing and able to pay the higher prices, reflecting inelastic demand for car ownership among certain segments. However, a noticeable dip in vehicle registrations from 2014 to 2016 coincides with a reduction in the number of COE quotas available, highlighting how government policy directly impacts car ownership levels. Overall, the trend illustrates that while economic and policy factors can influence short-term fluctuations, underlying demand for vehicles remains robust.

The line chart shows a general increase in both vehicle registrations and assessable income over time, suggesting a positive relationship between household income and car ownership in Singapore. As assessable income rises, more households are able to afford the costs associated with owning a car, including the COE premiums and related expenses, leading to higher vehicle registrations. The trend indicates that car ownership is closely tied to financial capability, and periods of stronger income growth are generally associated with increased demand for vehicles. This insight underscores the role of economic prosperity in driving consumer behavior in the automotive market.

The chart reveals dramatic volatility in PQP (Prevailing Quota Premium) values over the past decade. PQP experienced a sharp decline from 2014, reaching its minimum of approximately $33,800 in 2019—a drop of over 50% from earlier levels. This historic low likely reflects several converging factors: reduced vehicle demand during economic uncertainties (COVID-19 Pandemic), potential oversupply in the COE quota system, and shifting consumer preferences as ride-hailing services gained prominence in Singapore. However, from 2022 onwards, PQP surged dramatically, climbing to its maximum of $107,700 in 2023 before moderating slightly to $97,400 in 2024. This remarkable recovery—more than tripling from the 2019 lows—can be attributed to several key drivers: post-pandemic pent-up vehicle demand as economic activity resumed, significant tightening of COE supply as part of Singapore's vehicle population control measures, supply chain disruptions affecting new vehicle availability, and strong economic recovery boosting purchasing power.

The line chart highlights a clear shift in Singapore’s vehicle market, with registrations of fuel-based vehicles declining while electric vehicle (EV) registrations are on the rise. This trend reflects growing consumer preference for cleaner, more sustainable transportation options, likely influenced by environmental awareness, government incentives for EV adoption, and improvements in EV technology and infrastructure. The divergence between the two lines suggests a gradual transition away from traditional internal combustion engine vehicles toward electrification, signaling a long-term change in the composition of the vehicle fleet and a potential reduction in carbon emissions from private transport.

The line graphs of individual fuel types illustrate a clear shift in Singapore’s vehicle landscape. Diesel registrations show fluctuations between 2023 and 2024, while diesel-electric vehicles decline steadily and diesel-electric plug-in vehicles show only a slight increase. In contrast, fully electric cars are growing rapidly, indicating accelerating adoption of zero-emission vehicles. Traditional petrol and petrol-CNG vehicles are declining, whereas petrol-electric hybrid and petrol-electric plug-in vehicles are on the rise. Overall, these trends highlight a strong transition from conventional fuel vehicles toward hybrid and fully electric alternatives, reflecting both changing consumer preferences and the impact of policies encouraging cleaner, more sustainable vehicle choices.

The heatmap of yearly fuel-type growth highlights a significant decline in petrol vehicle registrations, while registrations for electric and petrol-electric vehicles show steady increases. This pattern indicates a clear shift in consumer preference away from traditional internal combustion engine vehicles toward more sustainable alternatives. The consistent growth in electric and hybrid vehicles suggests rising adoption driven by environmental awareness, technological advancements, and supportive government policies. Overall, the heatmap underscores a gradual but decisive transition in the vehicle market toward cleaner and more efficient fuel types.

Singapore’s car market demonstrates resilient demand sustained by rising incomes and constrained by government quota policies. While economic and policy factors introduce short-term volatility, the long-term upward trajectory of COE premiums and registrations reflects a persistent appetite for vehicle ownership, shaped by both affluence and regulatory design.

Who Does it Matter to?

Our group aims to develop an integrated COE Analytics Dashboard that provides comprehensive insights for stakeholders.

Buyers/Sellers

For Buyers, uncertainty arises in deciding the right time to purchase a car, whether to revalidate an existing vehicle, and which COE category offers the best value for their budget. For Sellers, on the other hand, struggle with fluctuating premiums that complicate the decision of when to sell in order to maximize value.

Car Dealers

Car dealers face difficulty anticipating demand for both new and used vehicles, as well as specific car models.

Government

Government must continually monitor quota utilization, manage price volatility, and ensure long-term vehicle population control so that the system remains sustainable and equitable.

Sentiment Analysis

To understand public perception beyond numerical trends, a sentiment analysis was conducted to uncover how emotions and opinions surrounding COE prices influence stakeholder reactions and engagement.

Sentiment Insights

Key insights from the Sentiment Analysis Dashboard.

The pie chart reveals that the majority of comments carry a negative sentiment, making up 65.37% of the total, while neutral comments account for 34.63%. This indicates that public sentiment leans largely negative, suggesting widespread dissatisfaction or concern among individuals regarding the topic discussed. The smaller proportion of neutral comments implies that few people hold indifferent or balanced views, highlighting the emotionally charged nature of the discussion. Overall, the sentiment distribution suggests that the issue evokes strong negative reactions, which could be useful for identifying underlying causes of public discontent or areas that require policy attention or communication efforts.

Around March 2025, sentiment dipped significantly — this likely reflects a period of heightened negative sentiment or concern about COE prices. One contributing factor could be that the prevailing quota premiums (“PQP”) showed little drop despite increasing COE supply, which may have frustrated buyers. For example, the PQP for Category A in March was S$92,525, with Category B at S$111,807. Moreover, the bidding results show COE premiums were still elevated in March (Category A ~S$94,502 on 19 March) despite expectations of relief. This mismatch between hoped‐for relief and actual pricing may have driven more negative comments in March. Then in April 2025, there is a sudden increase in the VADER compound (i.e., sentiment becomes more positive or less negative). In April, the COE results show mixed trends: Category A rose by S$1,776 to S$99,500 in the second bidding round, even as supply for upcoming quarter was announced to increase by more than 6% (which might have eased longer-term concerns) Also, the increased quota announcement may have signalled to the market that supply constraints might ease, improving sentiment. Despite the price staying high, the perception of improving supply and perhaps “price plateauing” may have given buyers more optimism — hence the uptick in VADER compound in April.

The scatter plot visualizes the relationship between sentiment intensity (VADER compound) and engagement (comment score). Overall, posts with more negative sentiment—represented by points on the left—tend to receive higher engagement levels, as seen from the cluster of high comment scores in the negative sentiment region. In contrast, posts with neutral or positive sentiment (toward the right) generally attract lower engagement, with fewer instances of highly rated comments. This pattern suggests that discussions surrounding COE prices tend to generate more attention and interaction when the sentiment expressed is negative, reflecting how emotionally charged or critical opinions often drive greater public response and engagement online.

Public sentiment on COE prices is largely negative, with over 65% of comments expressing frustration over high premiums and perceived market unfairness. Sentiment dipped sharply in March 2025 when expected price relief did not occur, then improved slightly in April following announcements of a higher COE quota. Negative opinions drive the most engagement, highlighting how consumer sentiment is closely tied to price perceptions and policy responses.

Key Drivers of Negative Sentiments

Analysis of the main factors contributing to negative sentiments around COE prices.

Key Insights from Drivers Analysis

The bubble chart illustrates the relationship between the intensity of negative sentiment and the perceived importance of various reasons behind it. While revenue utilisation concerns register the highest VADER compound score—indicating relatively milder negativity—they are viewed as the least important reason overall. Similarly, congestion and accessibility issues and elitist policy disconnection exhibit moderately high sentiment scores but are assigned lower importance, suggesting that while people express negativity toward these issues, they do not perceive them as the main causes of dissatisfaction. In contrast, systematic market inequity stands out as the most important reason yet carries the lowest VADER compound score, indicating stronger negativity and deeper frustration associated with fairness and equity concerns in the system. Overall, the chart highlights that while some issues evoke mild discontent, concerns over market inequity are the primary driver of strong negative sentiment among respondents.
