Seller Insights

  • Premium Projection: The COE premium trend from 2010–2024 shows pronounced cyclical fluctuations, peaking near $150,000 during 2024 after previous highs in 2013–2014. This reflects constrained quota supply and intensified post-pandemic demand from private owners and ride-hailing fleets.
  • Model projections beyond 2025 suggest a plateau between $120,000–$150,000 through 2035 — indicating that without major policy changes, COE prices will remain high. Sellers can expect strong resale leverage as replacement costs stay elevated.
  • Quota Forecast: Quotas rose sharply between 2015–2018 (peaking at around 1,350 certificates) before contracting to below 500 in 2022. Projections from 2025 onward show a modest recovery to about 700–800 certificates per period, followed by a slow decline.
  • This stabilization signals a low-supply equilibrium: deregistrations remain limited, keeping demand pressure high. As a result, COE premiums are projected to stay firm, reinforcing seller advantages in the used-car market.

  • The scatter plot reveals a clear negative correlation between COE quota and premium. When quotas increase, premiums tend to fall — and vice versa.
  • In recent high-premium years (2022–2024), quotas fell to under 600. In contrast, the lower-premium years (2016–2018) corresponded to quotas exceeding 1,200 certificates.
  • Regression lines confirm this inverse relationship for both Category A and B. Limited supply has been the key driver of elevated prices rather than over-demand alone.
  • For sellers: price drops are only likely if the government releases a substantial quota increase or if a large wave of deregistrations occurs. Until then, high COE premiums will sustain vehicle resale values.

  • The heat-score combines demand strength (bids per quota) and price momentum (COE rate of change) to measure how “hot” each market segment is.
  • Between 2024–2026, Category B shows the strongest demand — dark-red zones reflect aggressive bidding from luxury and private owners. Category A remains warm but comparatively stable, showing moderate demand and consistent turnover.
  • Historically, spikes in Category B heat intensity (2012–2014 and 2022) align with COE surges beyond $100,000. This reinforces the trend that Category B premiums react faster and stronger to market shocks.
  • Implication: Category B sellers currently hold the strongest leverage in the market, benefiting from both high resale prices and sustained buyer demand. Category A sellers can capitalize on faster transaction cycles given their affordability relative to Category B.

  • Resale values are expected to remain strong through 2030 as COE premiums stabilize near historical highs.
  • Sellers of Category B vehicles can secure peak returns in the next 1–2 years before potential quota normalization.
  • Monitor quota releases — any jump above 900 certificates per bidding window may trigger a 15%+ premium correction.
  • Timing strategy: Consider listing or trading vehicles before major deregistration waves, when premiums are at their peak and replacement costs are highest.
