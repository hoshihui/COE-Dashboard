The Government Dashboard provides insights to support policy planning and regulatory decisions under Singapore’s COE framework.It examines how quota allocation, price volatility, and vehicle electrification interact to shape market stability and long-term sustainability.These visual analytics help agencies like LTA and MOT assess whether current COE policies remain effective in controlling vehicle growth while advancing the nation’s transition toward electric mobility under the Singapore Green Plan 2030.By analysing quota utilisation, COE premium fluctuations, buyer responsiveness, and EV adoption trends, policymakers can better anticipate market behaviour, calibrate the “cut-and-fill” mechanism, and refine quota strategies to ensure both affordability and environmental progress.

Quota Utilization Efficiency Dashboard

Quota Utilization Efficiency Dashboard Insights

Singapore’s car market is shaped by COE quota policies. The top chart shows consistently high SuccessBid/Quota efficiency above 97% for both Category A and B vehicles from 2010–2025, indicating that nearly all COE quotas are fully utilized each year. Category B generally maintained slightly higher efficiency, reflecting stronger demand for larger or \luxury cars. The bottom chart, ReceivedBid/Quota, reveals the intensity of bidding competition — peaking sharply in 2013 (up to 199% for Category A) before stabilizing around 140–160% in recent years. This suggests that while overall quota utilization remains stable, bidding pressure fluctuates with market demand and quota supply changes.

Premium shifts lead PQP changes, creating windows where renewals can be cheaper than new bids. Tracking these gaps helps owners decide whether to renew or re-enter the bidding cycle.

Category A reacts faster to supply cuts, while Category B absorbs quota changes more steadily. Aligning releases across categories can smooth price spikes and help manage market expectations.

Volatility of Premiums Dashboard

Volatility of Premiums Dashboard Insights

The chart illustrates the volatility of COE premiums across 2024–2025. Category A premiums fluctuate more sharply than Category B, indicating greater market sensitivity and demand uncertainty for smaller cars. Sharp monthly swings (up to ±$10K) in Category A highlight higher volatility and reactive bidding behavior compared to the steadier trend in Category B.

Category A shows higher premium volatility as its price-sensitive buyers react strongly to COE price changes — bidding more when prices fall and pulling back when prices rise. This explains the negative correlation (≈ –1) between premiums and successful bids, while Category B’s steadier, less price-sensitive demand leads to lower volatility and occasional positive correlation.

** Negative correlation (close to -1) suggests higher success occurs when premiums fall.

EV Car Share and Projection Dashboard

EV Car Share and Projection Insights

From 2014 to 2024, Singapore’s car mix shifted notably toward electrification. Hybrid and Electric Base cars increased from just 7.4K vehicles (0.8%) in 2014 to 176K vehicles (14.7%) in 2024, while Fuel Base cars declined from 944K to 836K over the same period. Hybrid vehicles initially dominated the growth, but from 2021 onward, fully electric cars surged as charging infrastructure expanded and government rebates took effect.

The projection from 2025 to 2030 uses an Exponential Growth model, which assumes EV adoption rises at a consistent percentage rate over time. With the total vehicle base population held constant at 2024 levels, the combined Electric Base (Hybrid + Electric) share is forecast to climb rapidly — from ~17% (162K cars) in 2025 to over 60% (588K cars) by 2030 — illustrating Singapore’s accelerating transition toward sustainable mobility under a cut-and-fill vehicle policy.

A "cut-and-fill" policy is a strategy used to manage the supply and demand of Certificates of Entitlement (COEs) in Singapore by bringing forward future quota from peak years to fill current troughs in supply. This approach aims to reduce volatility in COE prices by creating a more balanced supply over time, although it involves a trade-off of potentially increasing the number of cars on the road in the short term due to this "borrowing from the future". 

How the policy works
• Cut: Reduces the COE supply in future peak years.
• Fill: Adds this reduced supply to the current supply troughs.
• Goal: To smooth out the peaks and troughs in COE supply, making it more consistent and predictable.
